Academic Projects

During my studies at the Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam, I have worked on a variety of academic projects that explore the intersection of technology and design. This page is a showcase of some of the most notable projects I have completed so far. And is continuously updated during my study.

Module 1

During the first module of my Creative Technology degree, I was introduced to the core disciplines and mindset of becoming a creative technologist. This module laid the foundation for my studies and helped me develop a strong understanding of the principles of design, technology, and innovation.

Poster Design

For a Visual Communication module focusing on "design for behavioral change," I was tasked with creating a poster that uses core design principles to convey a complex message clearly. I chose to address Phishing, aiming to create a visual cue that stops users before they fall victim to digital scams.

The Design Solution Using Adobe InDesign, I developed a strong visual metaphor, the fish hook to immediately communicate the danger of being deceived.

I utilized a combination of bold contrast, clear hierarchy and simplicity

Behavior Change Video

During a one-week intensive hackathon, we were asked to design a social marketing video aimed at influencing behavior. The goal was to combine storytelling, visual communication, and behavioral psychology to address a specific issue relevant to our peers.

My team chose to tackle the reliance on ChatGPT in academic studies. Instead of a standard warning, we used humor to illustrate the long-term dangers of AI usage. The video shows professionals such as an electrician, a surgeon, and a bomb squad technician failing because they relied on AI rather than their own knowledge.

Sense-Process-Act

My team and I designed and build a tangible prototype using the sense process act concept. We used the NE555 timer for this as the processing unit.

We developed an autonomous vehicle that follows a line on the ground. The car uses light sensors (sense) to detect the line and adjust its steering accordingly (act), using the NE555 processing unit. This technology was cased in a laser cut wooden car.

While the prototype encountered functional limitations during the final demonstration, the project was a valuable exercise in analog circuit design, hardware troubleshooting, and rapid prototyping under strict deadlines.

Smart Relaxation Room

For a smart environments project, we designed an interactive room that adapts to users' relaxation needs. The room uses sensors to monitor environmental factors like heartrate, external noise and temperature, adjusting them accordingly to create an optimal relaxation experience.

The room features adjustable lighting, soundscapes, and climate control, all orchestrated through a central system that responds to real-time data. This project highlights the potential of technology to enhance well-being through personalized environments.
